S.154 CrPC
⌂
First Information Report
The FIR is the formal gateway into the criminal justice process. Its evidentiary weight, the duty it imposes on the officer in charge, and the consequences of delay are all governed by how the information is classified at first contact.

S.497 CrPC
⌂
Bail in Non-Bailable Offences
Bail in a non-bailable offence is not automatic — it is a judicial discretion exercised against statutory exceptions. The forum and the test both shift depending on whether the case is still with the Sessions Court or has moved to the High Court.

Appeals & Revision
The appellate ladder runs upward through the ordinary criminal courts — Magistrate to Sessions to High Court — entirely within the CrPC. It does not continue into constitutional jurisdiction; that is a separate, parallel track.

Arrest & Warrants

S.96–103 CrPC
⌂
Search & Seizure
A search conducted under a warrant follows one procedural track; a search conducted by a police officer without prior judicial authorisation, during an investigation, follows a narrower one. Both converge on the same witness-and-record safeguards in S.103.

Investigation Powers
Investigation begins the moment an FIR is registered for a cognizable offence and runs until the police file a report under S.173. The S.167 remand timeline is the most exam-tested safeguard in this entire sequence.

S.190–204 CrPC
⌂
Complaints to Magistrate
A Magistrate may take cognizance of an offence by three distinct routes. A private complaint under S.200 is the most commonly tested — it triggers a specific sequence of examination, inquiry, and either dismissal or process, governed by S.200–204.

S.221–240 CrPC
⌂
Charge Framing
A charge is the formal accusation that defines the scope of a trial. Its contents, the power to alter it, and the consequences of error are governed by S.221–240 CrPC — the most technically demanding part of criminal procedure for exam purposes.

S.239–265 CrPC
⌂
Trial Tracks
CrPC provides three distinct trial tracks based on the gravity of the offence. The track determines the procedure from charge through judgment. Warrant cases and summons cases have structurally different sequences; summary trials are a fast-track for minor matters.

S.161–164 CrPC
⌂
Confessions & Statements
S.161 governs police examination of witnesses during investigation; S.164 governs recording of confessions and statements before a Magistrate. The evidential value of each, and the conditions under which they are admissible, are among the most heavily contested issues linking CrPC to the Qanun-e-Shahadat Order.

S.366–371 CrPC
⌂
Judgment & Sentencing
Judgment is the formal culmination of a trial. S.366–371 CrPC govern its form, the manner of its delivery, and the special procedure for death sentences. A judgment is not merely a verdict — it must contain the reasons that connect the evidence to the conclusion.
